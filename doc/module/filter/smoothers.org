#+TITLE: smoothers
#+OPTIONS: toc:nil num:nil ^:nil
#+STARTUP: showall

* Purpose

This family is the linear, frequency-shaping core of =nexenne::filter=: given a
stream of noisy samples you push in one at a time, each filter hands back a
cleaned-up or reshaped version of that stream. "Linear" means the output is a
weighted sum of inputs (and, for the recursive members, of past outputs), so
doubling the input doubles the output and the filter never invents frequencies
that were not already present. Use them to tame sensor jitter (=ema=, =sma=,
=lowpass=), to strip a slow DC drift off a measurement (=highpass=), or to carve
a precise shape out of the spectrum (=biquad=, =butterworth=, =fir=).

Every smoother here exposes the same small =filter_like= surface, so they are
interchangeable at the call site and you can swap one for another without
touching the surrounding loop:

- =push(sample)= : feed one input, get the filtered output back (=[[nodiscard]]=).
- =value()= : peek at the most recent output without advancing the filter.
- =reset()= : clear the internal state back to silence (coefficients are kept).

All of them are header-only, allocation-free, and =constexpr= where the math
allows. They are polled, never blocking, so they drop straight into a control
loop, an audio callback, or a bare-metal superloop.

* The idea

A digital filter computes each output from the inputs (and possibly the past
outputs) around it. The first fork in the road is whether it remembers its own
output:

- *FIR* (Finite Impulse Response): the output is a weighted sum of the last =N=
  inputs only, no feedback. A single spike in the input affects exactly =N=
  outputs and then is gone, hence "finite". Always stable (there is nothing to
  diverge), and if the weights are symmetric the filter has *linear phase*:
  every frequency is delayed by the same =(N-1)/2= samples, so the waveform
  shape is preserved. =fir= is the member here.

- *IIR* (Infinite Impulse Response): the output feeds back into itself, so a
  single input spike echoes forever (decaying, if the filter is stable). This
  buys a sharp response from very little state and arithmetic, at the cost of
  nonlinear phase and a stability you must respect. =ema=, =lowpass=,
  =highpass=, =biquad=, and =butterworth= are all IIR.

The recursive members come in rising order of sophistication:

- *Single-pole* (=ema=, =lowpass=, =highpass=): one feedback term, one delay
  element. The cheapest useful smoother, a gentle 6 dB-per-octave slope.
- *Moving average* (=sma=): the mean of the last =N= samples. Technically an
  FIR with all taps equal, but cheap to run incrementally; treated on its own
  because you reach for it by name.
- *Second-order biquad* (=biquad=): two poles and two zeros, five coefficients.
  One section can be a low-pass, high-pass, band-pass, or notch with a tunable
  resonance.
- *Butterworth* (=butterworth=): a cascade of biquads tuned for a *maximally
  flat* passband (no ripple) and a monotonic rolloff that steepens with order.

The single knob you trade against everywhere is *smoothing versus lag*. A
longer =ema= time constant, a wider =sma= window, a lower =lowpass= cutoff, or
more =fir= taps all reject more noise but also delay the signal more and react
more sluggishly to real changes. There is no free lunch: a filter that removes
noise without delay would have to predict the future.

For cutoff-based members the frequency knob is a real frequency in Hz, paired
with the *sample rate* (also Hz, the rate at which you call =push=). The two
together fix the filter's behaviour; the cutoff must stay below the *Nyquist*
frequency (half the sample rate), the highest frequency a sampled signal can
represent.

* How it works

** ema: exponential moving average

The exponential moving average blends each new sample with the running output:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
y[n] = alpha * x[n] + (1 - alpha) * y[n-1]
#+END_EXAMPLE

The single parameter =alpha= (the smoothing factor, in =(0, 1]=) sets how fast
old data is forgotten. =alpha = 1= disables smoothing (output equals input);
smaller =alpha= weights the history more heavily and smooths harder. This is
exactly a first-order low-pass with time constant =tau = (1 - alpha) / alpha=
samples. The weight of a sample =k= steps in the past decays as
=(1 - alpha)^k=, an exponential tail, which is where the name comes from.

The first =push= after construction (or =reset()=) *primes* the filter by
seeding =y= directly with that sample, so there is no slow ramp up from zero.
=reset()= drops back to the unprimed state; =reset(initial)= seeds a known
primed value instead. =alpha(a)= retunes on the fly without disturbing the
stored output. Cost is one multiply and one add per sample, no buffer.

** sma: simple moving average

The simple moving average outputs the arithmetic mean of the last =N= samples:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
y[n] = (x[n] + x[n-1] + ... + x[n-N+1]) / N
#+END_EXAMPLE

The window =N= is a compile-time template parameter, so the storage is a
=std::array= of =N= samples (zero heap). The running sum is kept incrementally:
on each =push= the oldest sample is subtracted and the newest added, so the
per-sample cost is =O(1)= regardless of =N=, not a re-scan of the window. Until
=N= samples have arrived it averages only what it has, so there is no startup
bias; =filled()= reports when the window is full and =count()= how many samples
it holds. =reset()= zeros the window and the sum.

A window of =N= introduces a delay of about =N/2= samples. Picture it sliding:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
input:   3   7   2   8   5   1   9   ...
                  +---------------+
window (N=4):     | 2   8   5   1 |  ->  mean = 4.0
                  +---------------+
next push (9):        +---------------+
                      | 8   5   1   9 |  ->  mean = 5.75
                      +---------------+
#+END_EXAMPLE

Equal weights make the =sma= a true FIR; if you need a runtime-sized window
without a buffer, an =ema= approximates it with exponential decay instead.

** lowpass: first-order IIR low-pass

This is the same recurrence as =ema=, but you specify a *cutoff frequency*
rather than =alpha=, so you think in Hz:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
dt    = 1 / sample_rate
RC    = 1 / (2 * pi * cutoff)
alpha = dt / (RC + dt)
y[n]  = alpha * x[n] + (1 - alpha) * y[n-1]
#+END_EXAMPLE

It is the digital twin of an analogue RC low-pass circuit: frequencies below the
cutoff pass through, frequencies above it are attenuated, with a gentle 6
dB-per-octave slope. The constructor computes =alpha= once from =cutoff_hz= and
=sample_rate_hz=, so per sample you again pay one multiply and one add. The
first =push= primes the value directly; =reset()= unprimes it while keeping the
coefficient; =cutoff(cutoff_hz, sample_rate_hz)= retunes without touching the
running state. If you already hold an =alpha=, reach for =ema= directly.

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
magnitude (dB)
  0 |--------\
            |\
 -3 |- - - - +\         <- cutoff (the -3 dB corner)
            |  \
            |   \__ 6 dB per octave
    +-------+------------> frequency
          cutoff
#+END_EXAMPLE

** highpass: first-order IIR high-pass

The complement of =lowpass=: it passes rapid changes and attenuates slow drift.

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
dt    = 1 / sample_rate
RC    = 1 / (2 * pi * cutoff)
alpha = RC / (RC + dt)
y[n]  = alpha * (y[n-1] + x[n] - x[n-1])
#+END_EXAMPLE

Note it keeps both the previous output =y[n-1]= and the previous input =x[n-1]=,
so the per-sample cost is one multiply and a couple of adds. The typical jobs
are removing a DC offset from a sensor, extracting the "AC component" of a
measurement, or crude edge detection in sampled data. The first =push= seeds the
input memory and emits zero (there is no prior input to difference against yet);
later samples apply the equation. =reset()= clears the input memory and output;
=cutoff()= retunes in place.

** biquad: general second-order section

The biquad is the Swiss-army knife: two poles, two zeros, five normalised
coefficients, realised in Direct Form I:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
y[n] = b0*x[n] + b1*x[n-1] + b2*x[n-2] - a1*y[n-1] - a2*y[n-2]
#+END_EXAMPLE

(=a0= is normalised to 1.) Different coefficient sets give low-pass, high-pass,
band-pass, notch, and more, all from this one recurrence with two input and two
output delay elements. You can set the five coefficients directly through the
=coefficients= struct (=b0, b1, b2, a1, a2=) via the constructor or =coefs(c)=,
and read them back with =coefs()=. =reset()= clears the four delay elements but
keeps the coefficients (so a coefficient swap with =coefs= mid-stream may cause a
transient, since the old delay line lives on).

In practice you rarely hand-compute coefficients: four static designers build the
standard Audio EQ Cookbook sets for you, each taking a frequency, the sample
rate, and a quality factor =q=:

- =biquad<T>::make_lowpass(cutoff_hz, sample_rate_hz, q)=
- =biquad<T>::make_highpass(cutoff_hz, sample_rate_hz, q)=
- =biquad<T>::make_bandpass(center_hz, sample_rate_hz, q)=
- =biquad<T>::make_notch(center_hz, sample_rate_hz, q)=

The =q= (quality factor) controls resonance and bandwidth. For low/high-pass,
=q = 0.7071= (= 1/sqrt(2)) gives the maximally flat Butterworth corner; higher
=q= peaks the response near the cutoff. For band-pass and notch, larger =q=
narrows the band. The low/high designers default to =0.7071=, the band-pass and
notch to =1=.

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
band-pass magnitude          notch (band-reject) magnitude
   ___/\___                     ----\    /----
      |                              \  /
   center                            \/
                                   center
#+END_EXAMPLE

** butterworth: cascaded maximally-flat IIR

A single biquad rolls off only so steeply. To get a sharper transition while
keeping the passband flat, cascade several biquads, each realising one
complex-conjugate pole pair. That is the Butterworth design: *maximally flat*
magnitude in the passband (no ripple) and a monotonic rolloff that gets steeper
with order.

=butterworth<T, SectionsN>= holds an array of =SectionsN= biquad sections; the
filter =order= is =2 * SectionsN= (each section is second order). =push= runs the
sample through every section in series, so the cost is =O(SectionsN)=. You design
it with one of:

- =design_low_pass(cutoff_hz, sample_rate_hz)=
- =design_high_pass(cutoff_hz, sample_rate_hz)=

These assign every section the same cutoff but a different per-section =q=, the
exact set =Q_k = 1 / (2 * sin((2k+1) * pi / (4N)))= that places the poles evenly
on the Butterworth circle. A =kind= enum (=low_pass=, =high_pass=) names the two
response shapes. For band-pass or band-stop, cascade a low-pass and a high-pass
at the two corners. Note that the design functions do *not* clear the section
state, so call =reset()= after a re-design to flush any transient.

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
magnitude                order 2 (1 section):  -.
  0 |======\__              order 4 (2 sections): \.
            \  \\           order 6 (3 sections):  \\.  steeper rolloff
 -3 |- - - - +. \\                                  with more sections
            |  \  \\
    +-------+-----------> frequency
          cutoff
#+END_EXAMPLE

** fir: finite impulse response

The FIR convolves the input with a fixed coefficient (tap) vector and uses no
feedback at all:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
y[n] = c[0]*x[n] + c[1]*x[n-1] + ... + c[N-1]*x[n-N+1]
#+END_EXAMPLE

Tap =0= weights the newest sample. Because there is no recursion it is always
stable, and if the taps are symmetric it has exact *linear phase* with a group
delay of =(N-1)/2= samples. =fir<T, N>= stores both the =N= taps and an =N=-slot
ring-buffer history in =std::array=, so it is heap-free. You supply the taps as a
=std::span<T const, N>= (a fixed-extent span, so the size is checked at compile
time) through the constructor or =coefficients(span)=, and read them back with
=coefficients()=. The coefficients themselves are designed offline, by a
windowed-sinc or Parks-McClellan tool, and pasted in. =push= stores the sample
and multiply-accumulates against the last =N= inputs, newest first, at =O(N)= per
sample. =reset()= zeros the history (the taps survive). The default-constructed
filter has all-zero taps and outputs zero until you supply coefficients.

The price is that =O(N)= work: a 64-tap FIR at 48 kHz is roughly 3 MFLOP/s, fine
on any modern MCU, but a biquad does far less for a similar magnitude response if
you can tolerate its nonlinear phase.

* When to use it

Reach for the cheapest member that meets the spec; only climb the ladder when the
response or phase demands it.

- =ema= :: The default lightweight smoother: one multiply and one add, no buffer,
  a runtime-tunable =alpha=. Use it when you want "less jitter, a little lag" and
  do not care about an exact cutoff. As a rule of thumb =alpha = 2 / (window + 1)=
  makes it mimic an =window=-sample average while keeping the =O(1)= state and time
  and zero storage, so it is the natural choice when the window would otherwise be
  large or runtime-sized.
- =sma= :: A true average with a hard, bounded window and predictable =N/2= delay.
  Pick it when you want every sample in the window to count equally (for example
  decimating or de-jittering a fixed burst), or when that exact, flat =N/2= group
  delay matters for aligning the output against another stream. Compile-time
  window, =O(1)= per push but =O(N)= storage; prefer =ema= when =N= is large or
  not known until run time.
- =lowpass= / =highpass= :: The =ema= idea (and its high-pass mirror) parameterised
  in Hz. Use these when you think in cutoff frequency and sample rate rather than
  raw =alpha=: noise rejection (=lowpass=) or DC-drift removal (=highpass=). The
  gentle 6 dB-per-octave slope means the cutoff is where attenuation begins, not a
  hard wall, so set it well below the noise you must kill and above the fastest
  signal you must keep. =O(1)=.
- =biquad= :: One designed second-order section: a sharper, resonant low/high-pass,
  or a band-pass / notch (for example rejecting 50/60 Hz mains hum). Use it when a
  single-pole slope is too gentle or you need a peak or a band. Tune with =q=:
  =0.7071= (= 1/sqrt(2)) is the maximally flat Butterworth corner, higher =q= adds
  a resonant peak near the cutoff, and for band-pass and notch a larger =q= narrows
  the band. =O(1)=.
- =butterworth= :: A biquad cascade for a steep yet ripple-free rolloff. Use it
  when a single biquad does not separate the bands cleanly enough; each added
  section steepens the rolloff by another 12 dB-per-octave but adds latency and
  per-sample work. Pay =O(SectionsN)= per sample for the extra order.
- =fir= :: The exact-response, always-stable, linear-phase option. Use it when
  waveform shape must be preserved (the phase matters) or you need a precisely
  specified magnitude response designed offline. The flip side is that the taps
  cannot be dialled in from a single Hz value at run time: you design them once in
  external tooling. Costs =O(N)= per sample; the only member here that is not
  =O(1)=.

All members are heap-free and ISR-safe; nothing here allocates or blocks. The
=O(1)= members (=ema=, =lowpass=, =highpass=, =biquad=, and per-sample =sma=) are
the natural fit for the tightest embedded budgets, while =fir= and the
=butterworth= cascade trade a little more arithmetic for a more exact response.
For the nonlinear, outlier-rejecting filters (a median, for instance) see
[[file:robust.org][robust]].

* Best practices and tuning

A few habits keep these filters well-behaved and predictable.

** Respect Nyquist

Every cutoff-based member (=lowpass=, =highpass=, =biquad=, =butterworth=) takes a
frequency in Hz alongside the sample rate. The cutoff must stay below the *Nyquist*
frequency =sample_rate / 2=, the highest frequency a sampled signal can represent.
Asking for a cutoff at or above Nyquist is meaningless and the coefficient math
degenerates. When the band you care about sits close to Nyquist, the cleanest fix
is to *oversample then filter*: sample faster than strictly required, run the
filter at that higher rate where the cutoff has room below Nyquist, and optionally
decimate afterward. This also pushes any analogue anti-aliasing requirement to an
easier corner.

** Separate the signal band from the noise band

A linear filter can only help when the signal and the noise live in different parts
of the spectrum. The whole tuning job is to place the transition between them: pick
=alpha= (=ema=), the window =N= (=sma=), or the cutoff (=lowpass=, =highpass=,
=biquad=, =butterworth=) so the band you want to keep passes and the band you want
to reject is attenuated. If the two overlap, no amount of tuning a linear smoother
will cleanly split them; you either move the bands apart (sample differently,
change the sensor) or reach for a nonlinear approach.

** Smoothing versus lag, concretely

The single knob you trade against everywhere is *smoothing versus lag*. More
smoothing always costs more delay: a lower =ema= =alpha= lengthens its time
constant =tau = (1 - alpha) / alpha= samples, a wider =sma= window adds its =N/2=
sample delay, a lower =lowpass= cutoff lengthens its =RC=, and more =fir= taps push
the linear-phase group delay =(N-1)/2= up. A filter that removed noise with no
delay would have to predict the future, so size the smoothing to the slowest you
can tolerate the output reacting, no more.

** Prefer cascaded second-order sections for IIR

A high-order IIR filter written as one direct-form recurrence is numerically
fragile: its coefficients span a wide dynamic range and small rounding errors in
them can shift the poles enough to ripple the passband or even lose stability. The
fix used by =butterworth= here is to factor the response into *cascaded
second-order sections* (biquads), one complex-conjugate pole pair each. Each
=biquad= is individually well-conditioned and easy to keep stable, and the cascade
realises the same overall response with far less sensitivity to coefficient
rounding. Build high-order IIR responses this way rather than as one big
direct-form filter.

** Choosing q

For the resonant designers, =q= (the quality factor) sets how the response behaves
near the corner. =q = 1/sqrt(2) = 0.7071= is the *maximally flat* (Butterworth)
corner: the passband stays as flat as possible right up to the cutoff. Raising =q=
above that adds a *resonant* peak near the cutoff (useful when you want to emphasise
a band, unwanted when you do not), while for band-pass and notch a larger =q=
narrows the band. The =biquad= low/high-pass designers default to =0.7071=; the
band-pass and notch default to =1=.

** Designing FIR taps offline

=fir= has no Hz-and-sample-rate constructor on purpose: its taps are *designed
offline* and pasted in. The two standard routes are the *windowed-sinc* method
(take the ideal brick-wall impulse response and taper it with a window) and the
*Parks-McClellan* (Remez) algorithm for an equiripple optimal fit to a target
magnitude response. Compute the =N= taps in external tooling, drop them into a
=std::array=, and hand the filter a =std::span<T const, N>=. Keep the taps symmetric
if you want the exact linear phase.

** Heap-free and ISR-safe by construction

Every member here is header-only, allocation-free, and non-blocking: state lives in
fixed-size members and =std::array=, =push= touches only that state, and nothing
locks or waits. That makes them safe to call from an interrupt service routine or a
bare-metal superloop. The =O(1)= members (=ema=, =lowpass=, =highpass=, =biquad=,
and per-sample =sma=) fit the tightest budgets, while =fir= and the =butterworth=
cascade trade a little more bounded arithmetic for a more exact response, still
without ever touching the heap.
