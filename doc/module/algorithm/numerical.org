#+OPTIONS: toc:nil num:nil ^:nil

* Purpose

Numerical routines that the standard library leaves out: accurate summation,
streaming statistics, root finding, quadrature, interpolation, ODE stepping,
and the FFT. The routines that can fail (root finders, FFT) report through
=std::expected<T, numerical_error>=; the rest are total functions.

- =kahan_sum= / =neumaier_sum= : compensated summation, far more accurate than
  naive accumulation at O(1) extra memory.
- =running_stats= / =histogram= / =ema_stats= : single-pass online statistics
  (Welford mean/variance, fixed-bucket histogram, exponentially-weighted mean).
- =bisection= / =newton= : scalar root finders, robust and fast respectively.
- =trapezoidal= / =simpson= / =gauss_legendre_5= : numerical integration.
- =lerp= and friends, =linear_interpolator=, =cubic_spline= : interpolation.
- =euler_step= / =rk4_step= / =verlet_step= / =velocity_verlet_step= : ODE and
  mechanics integrators.
- =fft= / =ifft= / =rfft= : the radix-2 FFT.

* How it works

** Accurate sums and online stats

Naive floating-point accumulation drifts as the running sum grows relative to
the addends. =kahan_sum= keeps a compensation term that re-injects the
low-order bits each addition drops; =neumaier_sum= refines it for addends
larger than the running sum. =running_stats= uses Welford's recurrence, which
updates the mean and the sum of squared deviations without ever forming a large
sum of squares, so it stays accurate when the mean dwarfs the spread; it also
merges two streams in closed form for parallel reduction.

** Root finding and quadrature

=bisection= needs a bracket whose endpoints straddle a sign change and halves it
each step, so it always converges but linearly. =newton= uses the derivative to
converge quadratically near a simple root, at the risk of diverging far from
one. For integration, the trapezoidal and Simpson rules sum weighted samples on
an even grid (second and fourth order); =gauss_legendre_5= places five samples
at the Gauss-Legendre nodes and is exact for polynomials up to degree 9, the
best accuracy per evaluation on smooth integrands.

** Interpolation and ODEs

The stateless eases (=lerp=, =smoothstep=, =catmull_rom=, ...) are single
expressions. =linear_interpolator= and =cubic_spline= store knots and binary
search for the bracketing segment; the spline solves a tridiagonal system once
(Thomas algorithm) for C2 continuity. The ODE steppers each advance one step:
=rk4_step= averages four derivative samples for fourth-order accuracy, while the
Verlet integrators are symplectic and conserve energy over long runs, which
plain Euler does not.

** FFT

=fft= is the in-place radix-2 Cooley-Tukey transform: a bit-reversal
permutation followed by log N butterfly stages, O(N log N). =ifft= reuses the
same machinery and divides by N. =rfft= exploits the Hermitian symmetry of
real input to do half the work, packing N real samples into N/2 complex ones.
Sizes must be powers of two.

* When to use it

- Summing many values where accuracy matters, or live mean/variance/quantiles
  over a stream: the sums and =running_stats= / =histogram=.
- Solving f(x) = 0: =bisection= when you have a bracket and need robustness,
  =newton= when you have a derivative and want speed.
- Integrating a function: =gauss_legendre_5= for smooth integrands,
  =simpson= for a fixed grid.
- Resampling a table or animating along a curve: the interpolators.
- Stepping a simulation: =rk4_step= for general ODEs, the Verlet steppers for
  energy-conserving mechanics.
- Spectral analysis: =fft= / =rfft=.

Confidence: the sums are checked on catastrophic-cancellation inputs; the root
finders and quadrature against closed-form answers (including Gauss-Legendre's
degree-9 exactness); =running_stats= against a two-pass reference with merge;
the FFT by round-trip, a naive-DFT differential, and known spectra.
